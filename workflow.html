Workflow

A simple process that keeps the science clear at every step.

The goal is to remove uncertainty for clients by making the intake, analysis, communication, and delivery stages easy to follow.

Best fit projects

Bulk RNA-seq studies, treatment comparisons, disease-control cohorts, time-course contrasts, pilot transcriptomics, and manuscript-focused reanalysis.

Step 1

Project intake

You share the study question, sample summary, metadata, organism, and current status of the dataset. This stage is also used to identify missing information before analysis starts.

Step 2

Scope and quote

A service scope is proposed based on sample complexity, number of contrasts, requested plots, and reporting depth. This keeps expectations aligned before work begins.

Step 3

Data review

Files and metadata are checked for completeness and coherence. If a design issue could affect interpretation, that is flagged early rather than buried in the final report.

Step 4

Analysis and interpretation

The core workflow is run and the biological story is developed around the strongest interpretable patterns, not just statistically significant output.

Step 5

Delivery package

You receive organized files, key plots, explanatory notes, and prioritized findings so you can move straight into discussion, validation, or writing.

Step 6

Revision and follow-up

Reasonable revisions are handled after you review the results with your team. This is often where extra value appears because questions become sharper after the first delivery.

What clients need to prepare

A smooth project starts with a clean handoff.

Preparing these items before the first call usually shortens turnaround and reduces back-and-forth.

Metadata sheet

Sample IDs, groups, time points, treatment labels, batch variables, and any clinically relevant annotations.

Study question

A short explanation of what you want to compare and what type of biological answer matters most to your team.

Existing files

Count matrices, FASTQ-derived summaries, alignment outputs, previous analyses, or draft figures if you already started.
